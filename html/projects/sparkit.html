Spark It

Dassault Systemes

Summary

Spark It is an educational web app that teaches electrical engineering concepts to kids. I designed and implemented UI elements for the front-end. I scripted and facilitated four user sessions, each with 6-12 kids.

Introduction

Dassault Systemes' main product SOLIDWORKS is a 3D modeling program that is primarily marketed to college students and trained engineers. Younger generations are not exposed to this software until high school or later. In response to this need, Dassault created SOLIDWORKS Apps for Kids, an entirely new suite of products that aims to bring these design tools to a younger demographic.

One of the Apps for Kids, Shape It, allows kids to sculpt complex 3D models.

Photo credit

My senior capstone project, which was sponsored by Dassault, was to build the newest addition to Apps for Kids: Spark It. The primary goal of this app is to teach kids electrical engineering concepts, like current and voltage. Due to NDA, I can't share full screenshots of Spark It, but I can explain the design process that I used to create the user interface.

User Research

Before our team built anything for the app, our team wanted to conduct user interviews to understand how kids think about circuits. As engineering students who have taken electrical engineering classes, we did not want to make assumptions about what kids did or did not already know. I looked into existing products in the market to see how other companies have been able to teach circuits to kids. Snap Circuits, Circuit Maze, and littleBits are examples of games where kids can build circuits from scratch, similar to the vision for Spark It. I wanted to see how kids interact with these games, in order to inform the design of our web app.

Snap Circuits is a circuit kit that comes with an instruction manual, containing over 100 different schematics that can be built with the provided components.

Photo credit

Our team invited a total of 18 kids to Olin College to play with these circuit games. I wrote user testing scripts to gather useful insights from these sessions. I asked the same questions to each user, so that I could more easily see the differences between people's responses.

For this user session, kids rotated around stations, each with a different game. In this picture, I'm kneeling and helping one of the kids run through the activity.

From this user research, I learned several key insights that drove the design of Spark It:

Kids need to understand fundamental concepts about electricity like current and voltage, in order to understand circuits.

Electricity is difficult to teach because it is invisible to the human eye.

Some kids thrive in structured learning environments, while others prefer to explore on their own.

Design

Our team decided that Spark It should allow kids to build actual circuits using batteries, bulbs, resistors, and other components. Whenever the user uses a component for the first time, a popup will appear to provide educational information. For instance, if the user adds a resistor to their circuit, a contextual popup will appear and explain what a resistor does.

The popup provides contextual information for each new component.

I wrote all of the text for each popup, and in order to make these concepts more understandable for kids, I compared electricity to the flow of water in a river. This analogy is intuitive because water is much easier to visualize than electricity.

A direct comparison between water flowing through a river (left) and electric charges flowing through a circuit (right).

Validation

After building a prototype, our team tested it with another 12 kids. For this user test, I wrote a script to get both qualitative and quantitative feedback about our app. I was most interested in determining if there was actually some educational value to Spark It. So, I wrote a pre-test and a post-test that kids would take before and after playing through our app.

For each test, we asked kids to tell us if the light bulb in each circuit would turn on or off. I based many of these questions around common misconceptions about circuits.

After playing the app, all 12 kids demonstrated improvement. They were able to learn new concepts about circuits that they never learned before. The test also revealed some of the problems with the wording in our app. For instance, some of the popups had too much information that didn't stick for many of the kids. One of the users skimmed the popup that explained the purpose of the battery, and when I asked her later about what a battery does, she responded, "The battery makes a connection to other stuff in the circuit." The vagueness of her answer implied that the popup needed to be simplified and re-worded.

Outcomes

After receiving all this useful feedback from user testing, I went through each of the popups and re-structured them to be more concise and understandable. I used more visual imagery and less text to convey each concept. Then, I documented all of my educational research before handing off Spark It to Dassault.

This is the new text and imagery I used to explain the concept of voltage.

At the end of the year, our team presented the final version of our app to the Olin SCOPE community. Afterwards, many adults played through our app and said even they learned something new. I eagerly look forward to see this app become fully incorporated into the Apps for Kids suite in the near future!
